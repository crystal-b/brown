Piet Mondrian in America or 'Boogie on Down'

In October 1940 the Dutch painter Piet Mondrian (1872–1944) arrived in New York, a fugitive from war–torn Europe. He spent his first few months in the city picking up contacts he had made earlier with American artists, dealers and critics. He also busied himself completing unfinished canvases that he had brought with him to the United States. But in 1942–3 he suddenly embarked on two entirely new and very large pictures that must have surprised even those friends and collectors already familiar with his work. Entitled Broadway Boogie–Woogie (pl. 15???) and Victory Boogie–Woogie, these paintings are in striking contrast to any he had created so far.01??? True, their compositional structure was still dominated by the same horizontal and vertical axes that had constituted the basis of his art since the early 1920s. Gone, however, were the dominant black lines whose cage–like grid had imprisoned planes of primary colour, allowing any possibility of escape only at the very edges of the canvas. Now colour itself forms lines, mostly yellow, running joyfully across the picture surface as if exulting in its new-found freedom, while some of the spaces between the lines are also occupied by larger coloured planes.02??? At the same time, the lines themselves are constantly punctuated by smaller rectangles of red, blue and grey–white. These set up their own contrasting rhythm, conveying an impression of irregular, syncopated movement. The effect is dynamic, exhilarating, even a little bewildering.

Mondrian's friend Charmion von Wiegand, who knew him well during the last years of his life, remembered the artist being 'terrifically impressed by the dynamism he found in New York' and that he 'wanted to make his paintings more dynamic'. She also recalled that Mondrian's earlier works now appeared to him 'too classically balanced once he looked at them with American eyes'.03??? Her remarks are to the point. The impression created by Broadway Boogie–Woogie and Victory Boogie–Woogie is certainly quite different from that produced by Mondrian's abstract colour compositions of the 1920s and 1930s, nearly all of which convey a feeling of repose or equilibrium. Whether we are really meant to associate his later paintings with the grid–like pattern of Manhattan's streets, the windows of skyscrapers and the lights of moving automobiles, as many commentators have suggested, is another question. Whatever else they might be, the pictures were obviously not intended as some kind of street map or aerial view of New York, least of all Broadway Boogie–Woogie. As Mona Hadler has emphasized, Broadway is the one diagonal that contradicts the otherwise relentless grid pattern of midtown Manhattan's streets, a disruptive element of which there is no trace in Mondrian's painting.04??? On the other hand, there is no denying that these works are far busier, far more expressive of movement and dynamism than any of his earlier pictures, as if the artist were seeking to express in abstract pictorial form something of the frenetic, pulsating life of the modern city.

At the same time, the irregular, constantly shifting patterns of squares and rectangles call irresistibly to mind the infectious, syncopated rhythms of jazz. This association is rendered all the more plausible by the titles given to these two late paintings, although the titles too are somewhat startling when considered within the verall context of Mondrian's pictorial oeuvre. Nearly all his works f the preceding two decades had been identified by names that are, in a sense, as unrevealing as possible, alluding to nothing outside of the picture itself. A typical Mondrian title would consist just of the word 'Composition' followed by a letter, a number or a listing of the colours employed: for example, his abstract–geometric Composition No. I of 1925 also known as Composition with Blue and Yellow. Admittedly, the more specific names given to some of the pictures he painted in America, such as the series New York City I – III, do have an immediate precedent in other 'urban' paintings begun shortly before his departure from Europe: Place de la Concorde, which he started in Paris in 1938, or Trafalgar Square, the product of his stay in London the following year. But the only antecedents for the Boogie–Woogie paintings, highly unusual among Mondrian's works inasmuch as they allude so specifically to another art form, are two canvases now owned by Yale University Art Gallery entitled Composition No. III, Fox–Trot B, with Black, Red, Blue and Yellow (1929 · fig. 88???) and Composition No. IV 1929/Fox–Trot A: Lozenge with Three Lines (1929–30).05???

These two work — or rather, their titles — alert the viewer to Mondrian's lifelong interest in music and especially popular music, a topic that crops up repeatedly in essays written at intervals throughout his career. He discussed questions to do with jazz and its relation to visual culture on several occasions: for example, in his essays 'Neo–Plasticism: its Realization in Music and Future Theatre' of 1922 and 'Jazz and Neo–Plastic' of 1927.06??? He also analyzed at some length the significance of Luigi Russolo's 'noise intoners', and of sounds and noises generally, in a two–part article published in the magazine De Stijl in August and September 1921.07??? This article was a response to a Futurist concert he had heard at the Théâtre des Champs­–Élysées in Paris, one of three performances given on 17, 20 and 24 June 1921, which had involved a large battery of intonarumori as well as an orchestra made up of more conventional instruments.08??? Press coverage reveals that these events were disrupted by noisy disturbances staged by Parisian Dadaists, with Andre Breton at their head. However, Mondrian's earliest extended discussion of music had already appeared in print the year before as part of a long and important essay entitled Neo–Plasiicism: the General Principle of Plastic Equivalence.09???

As its title suggests, Mondrian's essay on Neo–Plasticism is couched in highly abstract and at times even mystically tinged language. Much of it is taken up by a wide–ranging account of his philosophy of art: in particular, his deeply held conviction that the purpose of all the arts was to mirror the har­mony and equilibrium that underlay the chance appearances of nature. But having reviewed the latest achievements of contemporary painting, sculpture and architecture, towards the end of that more general discussion he turned his attention to other art forms including poetry and music. In Mondrian's eyes, one of the defining characteristics of the new music was a polar opposi­tion between melody and harmony, melody being characterized as 'descrip­tive' and 'emotional', harmony as 'orderly' and 'objective'. He writes about how the music of the past had mainly consisted of a 'confusion of the active and the passive' and about how what he termed the 'constructive plastic' was all too often 'veiled by descriptive melody'. The next and most urgent goal, Mondrian concluded, should be the 'demolition of melody': a task already being undertaken, albeit 'somewhat brutally', by the jazz band with its 'dry, unfamiliar, strange noises that oppose rounded sound'.10??? Just as important in this context was music's rhythmic aspect. In his article about Russolo's noise machines, Mondrian wrote that 'as soon as composition dominates, the rhythm becomes more absolute and the expression far more universal, as in olden times eg in fugues by Bach or in our own day in van Domseiaer's Experiments in Style'.11???

Today, Mondrian's allusion to the Dutch composer Jakob van Domselaer (1890–1960) requires some unpicking, given that the latter ulti­mately made little impact on the history of twentieth-century music. The two men first met in the autumn of 1912 and soon became close friends. As their relationship developed, it became apparent that they had a good deal in common: for example, their shared enthusiasm for the mystical mathematics propounded by the Dutch theosophist and teacher M. H. J. Schoenmaekers, whose book The New Image of the World (1915) was one of Mondrian's most cherished possessions. In a letter dated February 1916, the artist Theo van Doesburg (1883–1931) remarked on the fact that both Mondrian and van Domselaer appeared to be 'in the grip' of Schoenmaekers' theories.12??? They remained in contact until at least 1916, the year of van Domselaer's marriage, when the latter published a group of seven piano pieces entitled Proeven van Stijlkunst — the Experiments in Style that Mondrian refers to. One can only a sume that his friendship with van Domselaer caused Mondrian to start taking a closer interest in questions to do with contemporary music and with possible relationships between music and visual art. Certainly, in his essay on Neo-Plasticism he sought to relate van Domselaer's music to his own ideas about balance and harmony. He also wrote that in his opinion the composer's work showed the beginnings of a 'neutralizing opposition' between descriptive melody and 'another colour, less natural, another rhythm, more abstract'.13???

For his part, van Domselaer told Mondrian's friend, biographer and fellow painter Michel Seuphor (1901-1999), that he had tried to 'trans­late into music my impression of Mondrian, both the man and his works'. According to Seuphor, van Domselaer had specifically asked that his Proeven van Stijlkunst should be 'played in such a way that the static element ... be accentuated, while the movement (or the melody) remains peaceful and flowing'.14??? In striving for a balance between harmony and melody, he must have felt that he was creating a musical equivalent of paintings by Mondrian which, even as early as 1916, were already dominated by care­fully calculated juxtapositions of horizontal and vertical elements. Van Domselaer also stressed the idea that the melodic aspect of music should yield its traditionally dominant role to rhythm, an obvious point of contact with ideas advanced not only by Mondrian but also by van Doesburg. Togeth­er, these two were the major painters, theorists and co–founders of De Stijl.15???

As for Mondrian, he may have been responding co ideas about music already current among artists of the De Stijl circle. He may even have been influenced by van Doesburg who, as Nancy Troy has pointed out, had been painting 'musical' pictures and drawing comparisons with music for some time before Mondrian first used musical titles or publicly expressed an interest in the subject. In fact, van Doesburg's Rhythm of a Russian Dance (1918; see pl. 16???), in which rhythm and movement are reduced to a play of horizontal and vertical lines, precedes Mondrian's first known mention of dance or discussion of music in his own writings by about two years.17??? Between 1919 and 1921, van Doesburg also sought to apply ideas about music to the fields of architecture and applied art, proposing the use of 'triads' of primary and secondary colours in designs he made for the decoration of buildings by the architect J.J.P. Oud (1890–1963). And in his colour schemes for a residential block and agricultural school in the small Frisian town of Drachten, van Doesburg suggested applying musical proportions to the distribution of colours in the ratio eight parts blue to five parts red to three parts yellow, corresponding to the first inversion of a major triad: the eighth, fifth and third notes of the scale.17??? Again there are points of contact with ideas later advanced by Mondrian, who wrote in his essay on Neo–Plasticism in music and theatre of 1922 about the relationship between sounds and colours:

Both sound and noise will be manifested as Jundamencal sound and fundamental nonsound, like colour and noncolour ... in Neo–Plastic painting. Their number will be determined by practice: probably three sounds and three nonsounds ( as in paint­ing: red, blue,yellow; white, black gray).18???

A more famous example of van Doesburg applying musical principles to visual art can be found in his designs for a stained–glass window for the de Lange house in Alkmaar, Holland (1917; fig.89???). This window is in effect an abstract colour composition in three parts, the flanking sections of which are mirror images of one another. The upper half of each of these side windows is both a repetition and, at the same time, an inversion of the lower part, as if a vertical piece of paper had been first divided in two and then folded downwards upon itself. By contrast, the upper half of the central window is simply an upside–down version of the lower half, like turning a piece of paper the wrong way up. Individual parts of each window likewise form pat­terns that are repeated upside down or back to front. As many commentators have remarked, these compositional devices might be considered the visual counterpart of the thematic inversions and instances of retrograde motion found in a typical Bach fugue, as described in Chapter 5. Like Mondrian, van Doesburg willingly acknowledged Bach's mastery of musical form: for exam­ple, when he wrote:

Music has advanced further than painting as regards its means of expression. In music — e.g. that of Bach — the idea of unity or harmony is directly translated into musical relationships, transformed into sounds and measures: that is, with the help of musical means of expression and nothing else.

If some other element were to intrude into the music, e.g. a repeating melody or the use of a particular instrument in order to imitate some aspect of nature, that would no longer constitute absolute music.

Exactly the same principle can be applied to the other arts.18???

Van Doesburg's and also Mondrian's emphasis on the vertical as opposed to the horizontal aspect of music - that is, on harmony rather than mel­ody -probably owed a good deal to a book on contemporary music by the theoretician Paul Bekker entitled Neue Musik, published in 1919.20 Van Does burg's article 'Notes on the New Music', published in De Stijl in November the same year, is both a review and a useful summary of the ideas contained in Bekker's treatise. Bekker identifies two opposing tendencies in modern music, the first being the exploitation of quarter tones or other small­er intervals, resulting in an ever-greater emphasis on melody. The second tendency, which involves the use of larger intervals including the whole-tone scale such as we find in the works of Debussy, underlines music's harmonic aspect at the expense of melody. Regarding the latter, Bekker writes that the distinguishing characteristic of this sort of music is that it is 'fundamen­tally unrnelodic'. Elsewhere, he observes that the emphasis on the harmonic aspect corresponds to a 'predominantly vertical way of experiencing music'. 2' But van Doesburg did not content himself with merely summarizing Bekker's ideas. He went much further, seeking to relate them to the broader question of music's relationship to the visual arts and to the development of present-day culture and of mankind generally. He commented on the fact that, in his eyes, an increased emphasis on the harmonic aspect of contempo­rary music is 'neither an impoverishment of our melodic way of feeling nor a narrowing of music's means of creative expre sion; rather, it is the neces­sary consequence of the development from the melodic to the harmonic taking place within our own selves.' He also discussed what he consid­ered to be the 'ornamental' aspect of Classical and Romantic music, which had depended largely on melody, comparing the turn away from melody in modern music to the tendency towards abstraction in contemporary visual art:

What corresponds to the abandon­ment of the appearance of natural form in painting is the abandonment of melody in contemporary music ...This correspondence wich painting is all the more apposite, inasmuch as we may regard the melodic aspect of older music as the individual, as opposed to the harmonic aspect of the new music, which is universal.

In the same context, van Doesburg pointed to the contrast between the exaggerated chromaticism of older, melodic or 'Romantic' music and the use of 'primary, simple tones' in modern music, which he compared to the new painting with its concentration on primary colours.22???

Mondrian undoubtedly shared many of these ideas about the rela­tionship between music and visual art. He may even have taken a hand in formulating some of them. But as far as his own personal tastes were concerned, he was clearly not a great admirer of the latest trends in serious music. He may have met Arthur Honegger (1892-1955) in Paris, where he also briefly made the acquaintance of Edgard Varese (1883-1965). And he could scarcely have been unaware of the rhythmic innovations that characterized Stravinsky's recent works. But he took little trouble to pursue such acquaintances and rarely mentioned these or other contemporary compos­ers. Moreover, he soon came to regard van Domselaer's later music as being excessively melodic, despite his initial enthusiasm for the latter's Stijlproeve11. His real passion, attested to by numerous anecdotes as well as by his own statements, was for jazz and dance music. Michel Seuphor, who knew him well in Paris during the 1920s, wrote more than once about Mondrian's passion for dancing and remembered their going together to see the Revue Negre at the Theatre des Champs-Elysees. He also described how in 1929 the artist visited the circus on the Avenue Wagram every night for two whole weeks simply in order to hear the jazz band that played in the twenty­minute interval.

When Mondrian arrived in New York in October 1940, the city was in the grip of a jazz revival, one important element of which was the craze for boogie-woogie, a musical fashion imported from Chicago but which by then had also conquered New York. He seems to have responded with great intensity to this new musical environment. One of his first acquisitions on moving into his Manhattan studio was a gramophone on which he could play the latest jazz records. One should not underestimate the extraordinary influence that the gramophone exerted on artists at this time, coupled with the availability of an ever-greater variety of recorded music. It has even been suggested that the American painter Arthur Dove painted two pictures with the same title but of contrasting character, his Rhapsody in Blue Part I and II, simply because the recording of George Gershwin's (1898-1937) Rhapsody in Blue that he owned extended over two sides of a 78-rpm record, the first side being occupied by the opening allegro, the second by the finale.,. Friends of Mondrian like Harry Holtzman also recalled accompanying the artist to well­known jazz clubs like the Cafe Society Downtown, where they could enjoy the latest hits. '5 Mondrian, who at the age of nearly seventy must have been apprehensive about starting a new life in America, was clearly delighted at finding himself in a sympathetic milieu in which other artists were respond­ing just as enthusiastically to popular music. When, shortly after his arrival, he met the painter Stuart Davis (1892-1964), one of the first things they talked about was their shared passion for jazz, while Davis's wife, who some­times partnered Mondrian on the dance floor, commented approvingly on the latter's ability as a dancer.26???

According to Davis, Mondrian was quick to pronounce certain examples of boogie-woogie to be 'Pure' or 'The True Jazz'. As for the rest, it was 'worthy, provided an inferior status be granted' .'7 And in an interview in 1943 with James Johnson Sweeney, who was to organize the first big memo­rial exhibition following the painter's death in 1944, Mondrian declared:

True Boogie-Wvogie I conceive as homogeneous in intention with mine in paint­ing: destruction of melody, which is the equivalent of the destruction of natural appearance, and construction through the continuous opposition of pure means -dynamic rhythm.28???

Although Mondrian is talking about his new-found passion for boogie­woogie, the points he makes convey the same ideas that can be found in his earlier essays: that harmony (here referred to as 'construction') consists in the reconciliation of polar opposites, that music and visual art must serve the same ends, and that rhythm is to be equated with the 'abstract' element in music. Melody, considered 'descriptive', was by contrast too closely linked with emotion and thus with music's subjective aspect. Its equivalent in paint­ing lay in the mistaken attempt to capture the outward appearance of nature, whereas the real task of art was to 'create unity ... to follow not nature's aspect but what nature really is'.'9 Hence the 'destruction of melody', which Mondrian considered to be the particular achievement of jazz, had its coun­terpart in the 'destruction of natural appearance'.

For many Europeans visiting the United States, jazz -no matter in what form they encountered it - was an especially vivid aspect of their American experience. Among those visitors were artists and compos­ers like Francis Picabia or Darius Milhaud, who translated the infectious rhythms and colourful timbres of jazz into their own pictorial or musical language. Milhaud recalled nostalgically the jazz ensemble he had heard in Harlem in 1922 and pointed out that he had used the same combina­tion of seventeen solo instruments in his score for the ballet La Creation du Monde, which he described as 'providing the opportunity I had been waiting for to use those elements of jazz to which I had devoted so much study'.30 Even earlier, responding to the sights and sounds of New York, both Man Ray (1890-1976) and Albert Gleiizes (1881-1953) had produced visual images to which they gave the title Jazz: the former an airbrushed abstraction of 1919, the latter a more obviously figurative depiction of the same subject painted in 1915.31???

American artists, too, responded enthusiastically to contemporary popular music. As mentioned earlier, the painter Stuart Davis shared Mondrian's passion for jazz, which exerted a profound influence on his thinking not just about music but also about art. Davis bad been captivated by jazz at an early point in his career, describing it as 'a continuous source of inspira­tion in my work ... for the simple reason that I regard it as one American art which seemed to me to have the same quality of art that I found in the best European painting.'32 For him, however, it was not just a question of quality nor even one of simply turning to music as a source of inspiration. Instead, he believed that visual art could usefully emulate the formal qualities of this new kind of music. He encouraged other artists to study jazz closely in an effort to attain a comparable degree of rhythmic variety in painting, advis­ing Romare Bearden (19n-1988), for example, to listen to the freedom that characterized Earl Hines's piano playing.

But Davis was also conscious of the fact that beneath that free­dom could be found an underlying order. Writing in 1922 about points of resemblance between painting and jazz, he emphasized the way in which one particularly dominant element in a picture could make 'lack of order seem orderly in other elements, just as in a jazz band the powerful rhythm holds together the most unrelated excursions of the individual pieces'.31 He even envisaged a parallel between the constituent elements of music and those of painting, pointing to affinities between the 'tonal intervals of music' and what he saw as their counterpart in painting: 'intervals of tone, colour, contrast, size and direction'. In the same vein, discussing the large mural he painted in 1939 for Studio B of the New York radio station WNYC (fig. 90???), he described it as:

a harmonious design of shape, color and direction . . . a visual decoration which creates a mood in the spectator, just as a piece of music creates a mood, instead of giving some kind off actual information or instruction.34???
